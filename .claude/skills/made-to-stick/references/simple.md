# Simple: Finding the Core of Your Idea

Simplicity is the first and most critical principle in the SUCCESs framework. But simple does not mean dumbed down. It means finding the essential core of an idea and expressing it compactly enough that it is both easy to understand and profoundly useful. The art of simplicity is the art of ruthless prioritization. If you say three things, you say nothing.

## Commander's Intent

The U.S. Army teaches officers to write a "Commander's Intent" (CI) for every mission. It is a plain-language statement of the single most important outcome. If communications break down, if the plan falls apart, every soldier can still make good decisions because they know the CI.

### How Commander's Intent Works

1. A colonel plans a complex mission with dozens of moving parts
2. The plan rarely survives first contact with the enemy
3. But the CI does: "Secure the hilltop by dawn" or "Prevent enemy movement across the bridge"
4. Every decision made by every soldier is filtered through the CI

### Applying Commander's Intent to Your Work

| Context | Commander's Intent Example |
|---------|---------------------------|
| **Startup pitch** | "We make hiring decisions 10x faster" |
| **Product launch** | "Customers can go from signup to first value in under 5 minutes" |
| **Marketing campaign** | "Convince small business owners they are losing money every day they wait" |
| **Internal initiative** | "Reduce support ticket volume by 30% in Q2" |
| **Presentation** | "The audience should leave believing AI will replace their current workflow" |

### Exercise: Finding Your Commander's Intent

Ask yourself these questions in order:

1. If my audience remembers only ONE thing, what must it be?
2. If everything else in my plan fails, what single outcome matters most?
3. Can I say it in one sentence without jargon?
4. Would a new hire understand it on their first day?
5. Does it help people make decisions when I am not in the room?

## The Inverted Pyramid

Journalists have used the inverted pyramid for over a century. The most important information comes first. Every subsequent paragraph adds less critical detail. A reader who stops at any point has still received the core message.

### Structure

```
[CORE MESSAGE — the one thing]
   [Supporting detail #1 — most important context]
      [Supporting detail #2 — helpful but not essential]
         [Supporting detail #3 — nice to have]
            [Background — for the deeply interested]
```

### Why the Inverted Pyramid Works

- People scan, they do not read linearly
- Attention is frontloaded: you have 3-7 seconds
- Mobile screens show only the first 1-2 sentences
- Executives read the first line, not the last

### Before/After: Inverted Pyramid Applied

**Before (buried lead):**
> "After extensive research spanning 18 months across 4 countries, involving 2,300 participants and using a novel AI-based methodology, our team discovered that the average enterprise customer spends 40% of their workday on tasks that could be fully automated."

**After (inverted pyramid):**
> "Your team wastes 40% of their workday on tasks a machine could handle. We proved it across 2,300 employees in 4 countries over 18 months — and we built the tool that fixes it."

## Simplification Techniques

### Analogy

Analogies let you borrow someone else's schema. Instead of explaining your new idea from scratch, you connect it to something the audience already understands.

| New Concept | Analogy | Why It Works |
|-------------|---------|-------------|
| Slack (early days) | "IRC for your company, but friendly" | Technical audience understood IRC |
| Dropbox pitch | "iTunes for your files" | Everyone understood iTunes sync |
| Airbnb | "eBay for physical spaces" | eBay's model was well-known |
| Docker | "Shipping containers for software" | Physical metaphor made abstraction tangible |
| Stripe | "PayPal for developers" | Positioned in a known market with a twist |

**Rules for good analogies:**
- The source concept must be well-known to YOUR audience (not just you)
- The comparison should hold at the structural level, not just surface
- Acknowledge where the analogy breaks down before your audience does

### Generative Metaphor

A generative metaphor is more powerful than a regular analogy because it does not just explain — it generates behavior. It tells people what to do in novel situations.

**Example:** "Names, names, names" — This was the motto of a local newspaper editor. When reporters had to decide whether to cover a story, what angle to take, or what photo to run, the answer was always: prioritize local names. It was generative because it guided thousands of micro-decisions.

| Generative Metaphor | Organization | Decisions It Drives |
|---------------------|-------------|---------------------|
| "THE low-fare airline" | Southwest Airlines | Reject first-class seating, reject assigned seats, reject hub model |
| "Democracy dies in darkness" | Washington Post | Pursue investigative stories, resist access journalism |
| "Don't be evil" | Google (early) | Reject short-term revenue for user trust |
| "Move fast and break things" | Facebook (early) | Ship features before they are polished |

### Prioritization: The Forced Choice

Simplicity requires forced-ranking. If everything is important, nothing is.

**Exercise: The "If you could only..." test**

1. List all the things you want your message to communicate
2. Force yourself to choose ONE
3. Everything else becomes supporting evidence for that one thing

**Example for a project management SaaS:**
- Candidate messages: easy to use, powerful integrations, great reporting, saves time, team collaboration, affordable
- Forced choice: "Saves time"
- Everything else supports it: "Easy to use (saves learning time), great reporting (saves analysis time), integrations (saves switching time)"

## Schema Theory: Using What People Already Know

People do not learn from scratch. They attach new information to existing mental models (schemas). The more you can connect to what your audience already knows, the faster they understand.

### How to Use Schemas

| Strategy | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Build on existing knowledge** | "It's a CRM, but for recruiting" — CRM is a known schema |
| **Start with the familiar** | "You know how Excel lets you sort data? Now imagine it sorted itself." |
| **Use universally understood concepts** | Time, money, family, health — everyone has schemas for these |
| **Tap into professional schemas** | For developers: "It's like git, but for database migrations" |

### When Schemas Backfire

Schemas can also mislead. If you say "It's the Uber of healthcare," people may import the wrong assumptions (gig workers, surge pricing, regulatory problems). Choose your schema anchor carefully.

## Simple vs. Simplistic

There is a critical difference between simple and simplistic. Simple ideas retain their depth. Simplistic ideas lose it.

| Simplistic (Avoid) | Simple (Aim For) |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| "We help businesses" | "We cut payroll processing from 3 days to 3 hours" |
| "Our product is better" | "Customers switch to us after their second outage with [Competitor]" |
| "We use AI" | "Our AI reads your contracts and flags the clauses that cost you money" |
| "Improve your life" | "Sleep 2 hours more every night" |
| "Innovation platform" | "Test 10 ideas a week instead of 1 a month" |

**The test for simple vs. simplistic:**
- Does it help someone make a decision? (Simple)
- Could it apply to any company in the world? (Simplistic)
- Does it generate specific behavior? (Simple)
- Is it an empty platitude? (Simplistic)

## Before/After Examples

### Product Messaging

**Before:**
> "Our AI-driven, cloud-native platform leverages advanced machine learning algorithms and proprietary NLP models to deliver enterprise-grade content optimization solutions across multiple channels and touchpoints."

**After:**
> "Write an email. Our AI makes it twice as good in 10 seconds."

### Presentation Opening

**Before:**
> "Today I want to walk you through our Q3 strategic roadmap, covering the five key initiatives across product, engineering, marketing, and customer success, including timelines, dependencies, and resource allocation."

**After:**
> "We have one goal this quarter: cut churn in half. Everything I am about to show you exists to achieve that."

### Email Subject Lines

| Before | After |
|--------|-------|
| "Re: Follow-up on our conversation regarding potential partnership opportunities" | "One question about our partnership" |
| "Q3 Performance Review and Strategic Planning Update" | "We grew 40%. Here is how we do it again." |
| "Invitation to participate in beta testing of new feature" | "You are one of 50 people who get to try this first" |

### Healthcare Communication

**Before:**
> "Patients with hyperlipidemia should implement dietary modifications emphasizing reduced saturated fat intake and increased consumption of omega-3 fatty acids."

**After:**
> "Eat less fried food. Eat more fish. Your cholesterol will drop."

### Financial Services

**Before:**
> "Our diversified portfolio optimization strategy employs risk-adjusted return maximization across multiple asset classes."

> "We grow your money while you sleep, and we have done it for 20 years straight."

## Exercises for Finding the Core Message

### Exercise 1: The Cocktail Party Test

Imagine you are at a loud cocktail party. Someone asks what your product does. You have one sentence before they get distracted. Write that sentence. Now cut it in half.

### Exercise 2: The Grandparent Test

Explain your product to a grandparent who does not use technology. If you cannot do it, you are relying on jargon and insider knowledge. Rewrite until a non-expert gets it.

### Exercise 3: The Headline Test

Write the newspaper headline for your product. You have 8 words maximum. What makes the front page?

### Exercise 4: The "So What?" Ladder

1. Start with your current message
2. Ask "So what?" — write the answer
3. Ask "So what?" again — write the answer
4. Keep going until you hit something emotionally resonant
5. That is your core message

**Example:**
- "We use AI to analyze sales calls" — So what?
- "Managers see which reps need coaching" — So what?
- "Reps close more deals" — So what?
- "Your team hits quota every quarter" — That is the core.

### Exercise 5: The Forced Tradeoff

List every message you want to communicate. Now imagine you can only afford a billboard (7 words). Which message survives? That is your core.

## Testing Simplicity with Outsiders

The final test for simplicity is not your opinion — it is whether outsiders understand it.

### The 5-Person Test

1. Find 5 people who know nothing about your product
2. Show them your message for 10 seconds
3. Take the message away
4. Ask them to repeat what it said
5. If 4 out of 5 get the core right, you have achieved simplicity

### Red Flags That Your Message Is Not Simple Enough

- [ ] People ask "But what does it actually do?" after reading it
- [ ] You need a follow-up explanation
- [ ] Different people interpret it differently
- [ ] It sounds like it could describe any company in your category
- [ ] You use more than one comma in the core statement
- [ ] It requires industry knowledge to understand
- [ ] You are proud of how clever it sounds (clever is the enemy of clear)

### Simplicity Checklist

Before publishing any message, verify:

- [ ] There is ONE core message, not two or three
- [ ] A smart 12-year-old could understand it
- [ ] It uses concrete language, not abstractions
- [ ] It helps someone make a decision or take action
- [ ] It is compact — every word earns its place
- [ ] It uses analogy or schema to connect to existing knowledge
- [ ] It passes the 5-Person Test
- [ ] It is simple, not simplistic — it retains depth and meaning
